# How a Foreigner Buys Property in Belgrade: The Complete 2026 Walkthrough

If you're reading this in English and thinking about buying an apartment in Belgrade,
you've probably already hit the same wall most foreign buyers do: the information is
scattered, half of it is in Serbian, and the rest contradicts itself.

This guide fixes that. It walks you through the entire process — from "Am I even allowed
to buy?" to the day you get the keys — in plain English, the way I'd explain it to a
client sitting across my desk. I've spent 20 years in U.S. real estate and I'm a Serbian
native, so I'll tell you both how the system *works* and where people actually get tripped
up.

> **The short version:** Yes, most foreigners can buy an apartment or house in Belgrade.
> The process is straightforward once you understand three things — the reciprocity rule,
> the role of the notary, and clean title. We'll cover all three.

---

## Step 1: Confirm you're eligible to buy

Serbia lets foreigners buy real estate on the **principle of reciprocity**: if Serbian
citizens are allowed to buy property in *your* country, you're allowed to buy in Serbia.
For most of our clients — U.S., EU, UK, Canada, Australia citizens — the answer is yes.

What you **can** buy freely:
- Apartments
- Houses (residential)
- Commercial property — offices, shops, warehouses

What you **cannot** buy as a foreign individual:
- **Agricultural land** — reserved for Serbian citizens.

If your country doesn't have reciprocity with Serbia, there's a clean, completely legal
workaround: you form a Serbian company (a *d.o.o.*, similar to an LLC) and the company
buys the property. We explain that fully in our separate guide on the reciprocity rule.

> **First action:** Before you fall in love with a listing, confirm your reciprocity status.
> We do this check for you at the start so you never waste time on a property you can't close.

## Step 2: Get your paperwork and money ready

You don't need to be a resident to buy. You'll typically need:
- A valid passport.
- A Serbian tax identification number (**PIB** for a company, or a personal ID number /
  *evidencioni broj* for an individual — we arrange this).
- A local bank account (recommended; makes payment and later rental income far easier).

On the money side: be ready to show the **source of funds**. Banks and notaries apply
anti-money-laundering checks, so have documentation of where your purchase money comes from.

---

## Step 3: Find the property — and vet it properly

This is where foreign buyers lose the most money, so slow down here.

Two non-negotiable checks **before** you sign anything:
1. **Clean title in the cadastre (*katastar*).** The property must be properly registered
   to the seller, free of mortgages, liens, or disputes you're not aware of. Older
   buildings sometimes have *legalization* (permit) issues — these are fixable but you
   must know before you buy, not after.
2. **The building's legal status.** Confirm the unit and the building are legally built and
   registered. A beautiful apartment in a partially-unlegalized building is a future headache.

We run these checks with a real estate attorney as standard practice. If a seller or agent
resists a title check, that's your signal to walk away.

---

## Step 4: The preliminary contract and deposit

Once you've chosen a property and it checks out, the typical sequence is:
1. **Preliminary sale contract (*predugovor*)** — sets price, terms, and timeline.
2. **Deposit (*kapara*)** — commonly around 10%. This secures the deal; if the buyer backs
   out without cause they typically forfeit it, and if the seller backs out they typically
   owe it back doubled. Get the terms in writing and understood.

Everything should be in a contract you can actually read. We provide an English explanation
of every clause — you should never sign a Serbian-language document you don't fully understand.

---

## Step 5: The notarized sale contract

In Serbia the **public notary (*javni beležnik*)** is central to property transfers. The
final sale contract is signed and **certified (solemnized) before a notary**, who verifies
identities, the contract's legality, and the parties' understanding.

If you can't be in Belgrade for signing, you can grant a **Power of Attorney (*punomoćje*)**
to a trusted representative (often your attorney) to sign on your behalf — properly notarized
and, if signed abroad, apostilled. Many of our foreign clients close this way.

---

## Step 6: Payment, transfer tax, and registration

1. **Payment** is made per the contract — typically by bank transfer to the seller.
2. **Real estate transfer tax of 2.5%** applies to resale properties (the buyer usually pays).
   New-build purchases carry **VAT** instead of transfer tax — confirm which applies.
3. **Registration of ownership** in the cadastre (*katastar*) puts the property in your name.
   This is the step that legally makes you the owner — don't consider the deal done until
   it's registered.

---

## Step 7: After you own it — the ongoing costs

- **Annual property tax:** roughly **0.4%** of the property's value, paid yearly.
- **If you rent it out:** rental income is taxed at **20%**, with deductions for
  maintenance and mortgage interest.
- **If you sell later:** capital gains are taxed at **15%** — but **drop to zero if you've
  held the property for more than 10 years.** (More on using this to your advantage in our
  investor guide.)

- Prime, central, move-in-ready stock commands a premium — and that's exactly the segment

## The 6 mistakes we see foreign buyers make
1. **Skipping the title/cadastre check** to "move fast." Never.
2. **Signing Serbian-language documents they can't read.** Always get a clear English explanation.
3. **Assuming buying gives residency.** It supports a residency application — it isn't automatic.
4. **Forgetting the U.S.–Serbia tax angle.** There's no income-tax treaty; U.S. citizens
   should loop in a cross-border tax advisor early.
5. **Overlooking legalization status** on older buildings.
6. **Going it alone in a language and legal system they don't know** — when a bilingual
   agent costs nothing extra at market commission and saves the deal.
