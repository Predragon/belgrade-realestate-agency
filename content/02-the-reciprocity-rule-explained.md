# The Reciprocity Rule Explained: Can You Actually Buy Property in Serbia?

Of all the questions foreign buyers ask me, this is the first and most important:
**"Am I even allowed to buy?"**

The answer in Serbia hinges on a single concept — **reciprocity** — and it confuses almost
everyone the first time they hear it. So let's make it simple.

> **Reciprocity in one sentence:** If Serbian citizens are allowed to buy property in *your*
> country, then you, as a citizen of that country, are allowed to buy property in Serbia.

That's the whole principle. It's a two-way mirror. Serbia extends to foreigners the same
right their country extends to Serbs.

---

## Why Serbia uses reciprocity

Many countries restrict foreign land ownership in some way. Rather than publishing one fixed
list of "allowed" nationalities, Serbia ties the right to a fair, mutual test: *we'll treat
your citizens the way you treat ours.* It's simple in principle — the only work is confirming
where your specific country stands.

---

## The two ways reciprocity is established

Your eligibility is confirmed through one of two routes:

1. **A bilateral agreement (treaty).** Serbia and your country have a signed agreement
   regulating property ownership. If one exists and it permits it, you're clear.
2. **Factual (de facto) reciprocity.** No treaty exists, but in *practice* Serbian citizens
   are able to buy property in your country — so the right is recognized in fact.

For most Western buyers — **U.S., EU member states, UK, Canada, Australia** — reciprocity
exists and buying apartments, houses, and commercial property is routine. But "usually yes"
isn't "definitely yes for you," which is why we always confirm in writing at the start.

---

## What reciprocity lets you buy — and what it doesn't

Even with reciprocity confirmed, one category stays off-limits to foreign individuals:

| You CAN buy (with reciprocity) | You CANNOT buy (as a foreign individual) |
|---|---|
| Apartments | Agricultural land |
| Residential houses | |
| Offices, shops, warehouses (commercial) | |

Agricultural land is reserved for Serbian citizens. Everything most foreign buyers actually
want — a city apartment, a house, a commercial unit — is on the "can buy" side.

---

## What if your country doesn't have reciprocity?

This is the part that surprises people: **a lack of reciprocity is not a dead end.**

There is no legal obstacle to a foreigner **forming a Serbian company** — typically a
**d.o.o.** (*društvo s ograničenom odgovornošću*), the rough equivalent of an LLC — and
having **the company buy the property.**

Why it works: the company is a **Serbian legal entity**. Reciprocity is a rule about
*foreign individuals*; a Serbian company is domestic, so the rule simply doesn't apply. You
own the company; the company owns the property.

This structure is common and has side benefits:
- It works regardless of your nationality's reciprocity status.
- It can be cleaner for **investors** holding multiple properties.
- It supports business and **residency** pathways.

It also has trade-offs — accounting obligations, corporate tax considerations, and setup
cost — so it's worth a short conversation about whether direct purchase or a company is
right for *your* situation.

## Quick FAQ

**Does buying property give me Serbian residency?**
No — not automatically. But owning property *supports* a temporary-residence application.
Many of our clients buy and pursue residency together.

**Can I buy if I'm of Serbian descent / a returning citizen?**
If you're a Serbian citizen, reciprocity doesn't restrict you at all — you buy like any
local. Dual citizens included.

**Is the company route legal and legitimate?**
Yes. Forming a d.o.o. to hold property is a standard, lawful structure used by domestic and
foreign investors alike.

**Can my spouse and I buy jointly if only one of us has reciprocity?**
Possible, and the company route can also solve this cleanly — worth a quick case-by-case look.
