# Investing in Belgrade Property: How the 10-Year Capital Gains Exemption Works

Most foreign investors come to Belgrade for the headline numbers — solid rental yields, a
maturing market, prices still well below Western Europe. Those are real. But the quietest,
most powerful number in Serbian real estate is one most people overlook:

> **Hold a property in Serbia for more than 10 years, and you pay zero capital gains tax
> when you sell.**

For a long-term, buy-and-hold investor, that single rule changes the math. Let me show you
how I'd think about it — drawing on 20 years of underwriting deals in the U.S. and applied
to the Belgrade market.

---

## The tax picture for investors (2026)

| Tax | Rate | What it means for you |
|---|---:|---|
| **Capital gains** | **15%** | **Drops to 0% after a 10-year hold.** The core of the strategy. |
| Rental income | **20%** | Deductions allowed for maintenance, mortgage interest |
| Transfer tax (resale) | 2.5% | One-time, at purchase (new-builds carry VAT instead) |
| Annual property tax | ~0.4% | Recurring holding cost |

Compare that to capital gains regimes in much of the West, and you see why patient capital
likes Serbia: **the system actively rewards holding.**

---

## Why the 10-year rule matters so much

Imagine you buy a central Belgrade apartment for **€150,000** and, after a decade of steady
~5–6% annual appreciation, it's worth roughly **€245,000**. That's about **€95,000 of gain.**

- **Sell at year 9:** 15% capital gains tax ≈ **€14,250** owed.
- **Sell at year 10+:** capital gains tax = **€0.**

That ~€14k isn't a rounding error — it's a return *just for timing the hold correctly*. Stack
that across a small portfolio and the exemption becomes a deliberate strategy, not a
footnote. (Figures illustrative; appreciation isn't guaranteed.)

## The income side: rental yields while you hold

The 10-year exemption rewards patience — and rental income pays you to be patient. Belgrade's
prime, central, move-in-ready apartments are the most liquid rental stock, supported by:
- Remote workers and relocating professionals.
- A steady stream of business visitors and regional demand.
- Diaspora and lifestyle tenants.

Rental income is taxed at **20%**, but you deduct real expenses — maintenance, and mortgage
interest if you finance. Net yields vary by location and unit, and secondary cities like
**Novi Sad and Niš** can offer stronger yields than central Belgrade for investors willing
to look beyond the capital.

> **The combined play:** collect taxed-but-deductible rental income for 10+ years, then exit
> with **zero** capital gains tax. Income while you wait, tax-free appreciation when you sell.

## The market backdrop (2026)
- Belgrade average **~€2,600/m²**; median property **~€135,000**.
- Growth has **cooled to a healthy ~5–6%/year** — the market is maturing, not overheating
  (a good thing for a long-term holder; less bubble risk).
- Top new-build apartments in Belgrade are projected at **~7–9%** appreciation for 2026.
- Foreign demand concentrates in prime, ready-to-occupy stock — where it has an outsized
  effect on prices and liquidity.

A maturing, steadily-appreciating market is *exactly* the environment a 10-year strategy
wants. You're not betting on a moonshot; you're compounding a stable asset and letting the
tax code work for you.

## How investors structure the purchase

Two common routes — we'll help you pick:

1. **Buy as an individual.** Simplest. Works well for a single property and a clean
   reciprocity status. The 10-year exemption applies to individuals.
2. **Buy through a Serbian company (d.o.o.).** Preferred by some investors holding **multiple
   properties**, those who lack reciprocity, or those combining property with business and
   residency goals. Note: corporate ownership has its own tax treatment and accounting
   obligations — and the individual 10-year exemption works differently for companies, so
   this is a decision to make deliberately, with advice.

**Important for U.S. investors:** there is **no U.S.–Serbia income-tax treaty**, so you'll
want a cross-border tax advisor to coordinate Serbian and U.S. obligations and avoid
double taxation. We make that introduction as part of our service.

## A simple framework for evaluating a Belgrade investment
When I underwrite a unit for a client, I look at:
1. **Location & liquidity** — central, foreign-friendly, easy to rent *and* easy to resell.
2. **Clean title & legalization** — non-negotiable; we verify the cadastre before you commit.
3. **Yield** — realistic net rental income after the 20% tax and real expenses.
4. **Hold horizon** — is 10+ years realistic for you? If yes, the exemption is yours to capture.
5. **Exit demand** — who buys this from you in 10 years? Prime central stock has the deepest buyer pool.

## Common investor mistakes
- **Chasing the highest yield in a hard-to-resell location.** Liquidity matters at exit.
- **Ignoring legalization/title status** on cheaper older units.
- **Forgetting the U.S. tax side** until it's a problem.
- **Selling at year 8 or 9** and handing the tax office money you didn't have to pay.
- **Buying without a local manager** when you live abroad — vacancies and maintenance eat returns.
