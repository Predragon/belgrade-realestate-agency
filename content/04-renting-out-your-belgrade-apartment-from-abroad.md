# Renting Out Your Belgrade Apartment From Abroad: A Foreign Owner's Guide

You bought in Belgrade — smart. Now the apartment sits empty while you're in New York,
London, or Frankfurt, and every empty month is money you're not earning. The good news:
renting it out from abroad is entirely doable, and done right it turns a holding cost into
a steady income stream while your asset appreciates toward that 10-year tax-free exit.

Here's exactly how it works, what it costs, and what you can hand off so you're not fielding
a midnight call about a broken boiler from a different time zone.

> **The short version:** Decide long-term vs. short-term, register and tax the income
> properly (20%, with deductions), and use a local manager so distance and language never
> become the problem. We'll walk through each.

---

## Step 1: Choose your rental model

| | **Long-term (12-month lease)** | **Short-term (furnished / nightly)** |
|---|---|---|
| Income | Steady, predictable | Higher gross potential, more volatile |
| Effort | Low — one tenant, occasional issues | High — turnovers, cleaning, guest comms |
| Furnishing | Optional | Required, to a good standard |
| Best for | Hands-off owners abroad | Central, tourist-friendly units with active management |
| Risk | Vacancy between tenants | Seasonality, regulation, platform dependence |

For most owners living overseas, **long-term leasing** is the lower-stress choice and the
one we usually recommend to start. Short-term can out-earn it on the right central unit —
but only with real management behind it. We'll model both for your specific apartment.

---

## Step 2: Get the legal and tax side right

This is the part owners abroad most often get wrong. Two essentials:

1. **A proper written lease.** It protects you on rent, deposit, duration, maintenance
   responsibility, and termination. We provide leases with a clear English explanation so
   you know exactly what you're signing.
2. **Declare and pay tax on the rental income.** Rental income in Serbia is taxed at
   **20%**, and you can deduct legitimate expenses — **maintenance** and **mortgage interest**
   among them — which meaningfully lowers the taxable base.

**For U.S. owners:** there is **no U.S.–Serbia income-tax treaty**, so Serbian rental income
also interacts with your U.S. filing. Coordinate with a cross-border tax advisor (we make
the introduction) so you report correctly in both countries and don't pay twice.

> Skipping registration to "keep it simple" isn't simple — it's a liability. Do it properly
> from month one; the deductions make the 20% far less painful than it sounds.

---

## Step 3: Set the right rent (and the right tenant)

Belgrade's most rentable stock is **central, move-in-ready, and well-presented.** Pricing
too high means weeks of vacancy that wipe out the premium; pricing right means a quick let
to a reliable tenant. We benchmark your unit against live comparable listings, factor in
furnishing and location, and target the rent that maximizes *net annual* income — not the
flashy headline number that sits empty.

Tenant quality matters as much as price when you're abroad: screening, a sensible deposit,
and a clear lease prevent 90% of the problems that distance makes hard to solve.

---

## Step 4: Decide what you'll manage — and what you'll hand off

Living abroad, the question isn't *can* you self-manage (technically yes) — it's whether you
want these landing in your inbox across a 6–9 hour time difference:

- Marketing the unit and screening applicants
- Lease signing and deposit handling
- Rent collection and chasing late payment
- Routine maintenance and emergency repairs (the burst pipe, the dead water heater)
- Utility/bill coordination and building (*kućni savet*) matters
- Periodic inspections and condition reporting
- Tax-relevant record-keeping for your accountant
- Turnover, cleaning, and re-letting between tenants

A local manager exists precisely so none of that depends on you being awake in Belgrade.

---

## What rental management costs

Typical fees in the Belgrade market:
- **Long-term management:** around **8–12% of the monthly rent**, recurring.
- **Tenant placement only (you self-manage after):** roughly **one month's rent**, one-off.
- **Short-term/furnished management:** higher (more work per booking), often a larger
  percentage of nightly revenue.

The math that matters: a good manager typically **pays for itself** by reducing vacancy,
pricing correctly, catching small maintenance before it becomes expensive, and keeping good
tenants longer. An empty month costs you far more than a year of management fees.

## Common mistakes owners abroad make
1. **Leaving it empty "until I figure it out."** Every vacant month is pure loss.
2. **Not declaring rental income.** A liability, not a shortcut.
3. **Forgetting the U.S. side** until tax season is a crisis.
4. **Self-managing across time zones** and missing small issues that grow into big bills.
5. **Pricing on hope, not comps** — and sitting vacant as a result.
6. **No proper lease or deposit** — then no recourse when something goes wrong.
