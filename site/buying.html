How a Foreigner Buys Property in Belgrade

From "Am I even allowed to buy?" to the day you get the keys — the entire process in plain English.

If you're reading this in English and thinking about buying an apartment in Belgrade, you've probably hit the same wall most foreign buyers do: the information is scattered, half of it is in Serbian, and the rest contradicts itself. This guide fixes that.

It walks you through the entire process the way I'd explain it to a client across my desk. I've spent 20 years in U.S. real estate and I'm a Serbian native, so I'll tell you both how the system works and where people actually get tripped up.

The short version: Yes, most foreigners can buy an apartment or house in Belgrade. The process is straightforward once you understand three things — the reciprocity rule, the role of the notary, and clean title. We cover all three.

Step 1 · Confirm you're eligible to buy

Serbia lets foreigners buy on the principle of reciprocity: if Serbian citizens can buy property in your country, you can buy in Serbia. For most of our clients — U.S., EU, UK, Canada, Australia — the answer is yes.

✓ Apartments ✓ Residential houses ✓ Offices, shops, warehouses ✗ Agricultural land (citizens only)

No reciprocity? There's a clean, legal workaround: you form a Serbian company (a d.o.o.) and the company buys the property. See our reciprocity guide.

Step 2 · Get your paperwork and money ready

You don't need to be a resident to buy. You'll typically need a valid passport, a Serbian tax ID number (we arrange this), and ideally a local bank account. Be ready to document your source of funds — banks and notaries apply anti-money-laundering checks.

Step 3 · Find the property — and vet it properly

This is where foreign buyers lose the most money, so slow down. Two non-negotiable checks before you sign anything:

  1. Clean title in the cadastre (katastar). Properly registered to the seller, free of mortgages, liens, or disputes. Older buildings sometimes have legalization (permit) issues — fixable, but know before you buy.
  2. The building's legal status. Confirm the unit and building are legally built and registered.

Step 4 · The preliminary contract and deposit

Once a property checks out: a preliminary sale contract (predugovor) sets price, terms, and timeline, and a deposit (kapara) — commonly ~10% — secures the deal. We provide an English explanation of every clause. Never sign a Serbian-language document you don't fully understand.

Step 5 · The notarized sale contract

In Serbia the public notary (javni beležnik) is central. The final contract is certified before a notary, who verifies identities and legality. Can't be in Belgrade? Grant a Power of Attorney to a trusted representative (often your attorney) — many of our foreign clients close this way.

Step 6 · Payment, transfer tax, and registration

  • Payment per the contract, typically by bank transfer.
  • 2.5% transfer tax on resale (buyer usually pays); new-builds carry VAT instead.
  • Registration in the cadastre puts the property in your name — the step that legally makes you the owner.

Step 7 · After you own it — the ongoing costs

~0.4% annual property tax 20% tax on rental income (with deductions) 15% → 0% capital gains after a 10-year hold

The 6 mistakes we see foreign buyers make

  1. Skipping the title/cadastre check to "move fast." Never.
  2. Signing Serbian-language documents they can't read.
  3. Assuming buying gives automatic residency (it supports an application, not automatic).
  4. Forgetting the U.S.–Serbia tax angle — there's no income-tax treaty.
  5. Overlooking legalization status on older buildings.
  6. Going it alone in a language and legal system they don't know.
