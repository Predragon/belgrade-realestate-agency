Renting Out Your Belgrade Apartment From Abroad

Turn a holding cost into a steady income stream — without fielding a midnight call from a different time zone.

You bought in Belgrade — smart. Now the apartment sits empty while you're in New York, London, or Frankfurt, and every empty month is money you're not earning. Done right, renting it out from abroad is entirely doable.

The short version: Decide long-term vs. short-term, register and tax the income properly (20%, with deductions), and use a local manager so distance and language never become the problem.

Step 1 · Choose your rental model

Long-term (12-month lease)Short-term (furnished / nightly)
IncomeSteady, predictableHigher gross potential, more volatile
EffortLow — one tenantHigh — turnovers, cleaning, guest comms
FurnishingOptionalRequired, to a good standard
Best forHands-off owners abroadCentral units with active management

For most owners overseas, long-term leasing is the lower-stress choice. Short-term can out-earn it on the right central unit — but only with real management behind it.

Step 2 · Get the legal and tax side right

  1. A proper written lease — protecting rent, deposit, duration, maintenance, and termination. We provide leases with a clear English explanation.
  2. Declare and pay tax. Rental income is taxed at 20%, and you deduct legitimate expenses — maintenance and mortgage interest among them — which lowers the taxable base.

For U.S. owners: there's no U.S.–Serbia income-tax treaty, so Serbian rental income also interacts with your U.S. filing. Coordinate with a cross-border tax advisor (we make the introduction). See our tax comparison.

Step 3 · Set the right rent

Belgrade's most rentable stock is central, move-in-ready, and well-presented. Price too high and weeks of vacancy wipe out the premium; price right and you let quickly to a reliable tenant. We benchmark against live comparables and target the rent that maximizes net annual income — not the flashy headline that sits empty.

Step 4 · Decide what to hand off

Living abroad, the question isn't whether you can self-manage — it's whether you want these landing in your inbox across a 6–9 hour time difference:

  • Marketing and tenant screening
  • Lease signing and deposit handling
  • Rent collection and chasing late payment
  • Routine maintenance and emergency repairs
  • Utilities and building (kućni savet) matters
  • Inspections, condition reports, and tax-ready records
  • Turnover, cleaning, and re-letting

What rental management costs

8–12% of monthly rent — long-term management ~1 month's rent — tenant placement only Higher % — short-term/furnished

A good manager typically pays for itself by reducing vacancy, pricing correctly, catching small maintenance early, and keeping good tenants longer. An empty month costs more than a year of management fees.

A realistic income picture (illustrative)

Central apartment, long-term let, €600/month → €7,200/year gross. Less the 20% rental tax (after deductible expenses) and ~10% management — you net a steady stream while the asset appreciates ~5–6%/year toward the zero capital-gains mark at 10 years. Income while you hold, tax-free gain when you sell. (Figures illustrative; actual rent and yield vary.)
