U.S. vs. Serbia Real Estate Taxes

Where Serbia wins, where the U.S. wins, and the one cross-border catch that trips up almost every American buyer.

If you've invested in U.S. real estate, you know the tax code is half the game. So the natural question when you look at Belgrade is: how does the math actually compare? Here's the honest, head-to-head version.

The short version: The U.S. rewards leverage, deductions, and deferral — and is complex. Serbia rewards simplicity and patience, with low flat rates and one standout benefit: zero capital gains tax after a 10-year hold.

Two different philosophies

The U.S. — rewards leverage, deductions, and deferral

The power isn't low rates — it's the machinery. You depreciate the building against rent you collect, deduct mortgage interest, defer gains indefinitely with 1031 exchanges, and your heirs get a stepped-up basis that erases the gain at death. The price of admission is complexity and, in many states, high carry.

Serbia — rewards simplicity and patience

Low, flat rates, modest transaction costs, and one beautifully simple feature: hold longer than 10 years and pay zero capital gains tax. No depreciation game, no 1031 — but no army of CPAs needed either.

The cross-border catch every American must know

There is no U.S.–Serbia income tax treaty. The U.S. taxes worldwide income, so Serbian rental income and gains are also reportable on your U.S. return. Without a treaty you rely on the foreign tax credit — and the mechanics don't always line up. The clearest example: Serbia's 0% gain after 10 years means there's no Serbian tax to credit, so the U.S. may still tax that gain.

The Serbian side can be wonderfully simple, but the U.S. side never sleeps. Work with a cross-border tax advisor from the start — we make that introduction.

A worked illustration (buy-and-hold)

Buy at €150,000, hold 11 years, sell at €245,000 — a €95,000 gain.

  • Serbian tax on the gain: €0 (held over 10 years).
  • A comparable U.S. sale: 15–20% federal + 3.8% NIIT + state — potentially €15,000–€25,000+ unless deferred via 1031 or sheltered.
  • U.S. citizen, the catch: because Serbia charges nothing, there's no foreign credit, so the gain may still face U.S. tax.
